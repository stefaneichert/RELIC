**How does RELIC look at the Christianization of Europe?**

*2026.04.16*

Some of the most powerful images depicting the European Middle Ages in our minds are Christian. The Crusaders marching towards the Holy Land. The cathedrals we visit in almost any major city, be it Notre Dame in Paris, Stephansdom in Vienna and so on, and so on. The world holds its breath every time a new pope is elected by the Conclave. This shows how truly successful the Christianisation of Europe was. But this was far from a straightforward process, taking multiple turns and adaptations.

![image](/news/ImageNews1.jpg "Text to show on mouseover")

The deep entanglement of politics and religion and the impact of political messaging by the Church are timeless phenomena. Many historians and social scientists will point out that this was fundamental for the “making of Europe”. They examine the historical transformation of different regions, both within and beyond the frontiers of the Roman Empire, and highlight the role of local bishoprics, monastic centres, and princely courts in the advance of Christianity. These models emphasised the dual nature of Christianisation with respect to top-down and bottom-up processes, but ultimately focused predominantly on the upper levels of society.

RELIC starts from a simple point: if we want to understand Christianisation as a historical process, we have to treat non-elite practice and local structures as central evidence, not as background. Our central question is what do we really know about this process from the perspective of the general population across the region? How was Christianisation received, and how did it actually unfold in rural spheres where most people lived? Such questions are not easy to answer. To make things even more challenging, there are contrasting historical and archaeological views focusing on different evidence and levels of analysis. We suggest a shift in the focus and the methodology is necessary.

The project centres on the eastern fringes of the Ottonian empire and builds a data infrastructure that enables the comparative analysis of the rural populations in regions affected by the expansion of Christendom. At the core are the newly emerged Christian monarchies whose parallel emergence implies similar trajectories. However, the project will also look at regions that did not form a long-lasting independent polity or experienced more gradual conversions. The missionary activities and overall cultural influence exercised by the Salzburg Archbishopric or Byzantium were important factors in their inclusion in the Christian West.

With all these overlaps between competitive religious and political elites, it is easy to forget the complexity of communal Christianisation against individual conversion. We know that long-lasting religious transformation can materialise in settlement formation, the expansion of the church network, the changes in burial customs, and the spread of liturgical objects. Now we can examine these processes simultaneously by collecting them in a unified multi-level database that allows us to track them over space and time.

In other words, through the spatial configuration of local churches, cemeteries, infrastructure, and physical environment, we reconstruct the social and religious networks of the common people, who, in our understanding, are the key element in the success of Christianisation.

Understanding the circumstances of the rural population and the local church organisation in the stabilisation of Christianity opens numerous possibilities. Different regional patterns will point to different dynamics, hinting at the respective causes, for example, different levels of influence of the Church and the secular state, the problems of centralised coordination, of gradual or rapid expansions, how this process potentially influenced the later reorganisation of social structures, of settlement networks and their nucleation processes, how these dynamics of a pagan to Christian shift appear in the archaeological material, what regional patterns are observable and whether differences in political and ecclesiastical independence overlap/correlate with patterns in the development of the early local church network. Answers to these wider questions depend on offering a ‘view from below’ on major political and religious processes.
